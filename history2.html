Overview: The internet as we know it today is a vast, interconnected network that has transformed how we communicate, work, learn, and interact. But it wasn't always this way. It has been around for longer than you might think.

Origins and Standards

The history of the Internet begins in 1969 with ARPANET, a network created by the U.S. Department of Defense to maintain communications during the Cold War. The first message between computers (from UCLA to Stanford) only managed to transmit “LO” before crashing—yet it marked the start of a revolution. The first email was sent way back in 1971, and computers first started to digitally share information in 1983. The true turning point came in 1989, when Tim Berners-Lee invented the World Wide Web at CERN in Switzerland. This was instrumental in making the Internet a useful tool for the public. Not only did it create the space for web browsers and websites to exist, but it paved the way for people to access visual media online. In 1991, The World Wide Web was publicly released, enabling people to share and access documents and websites through a standardized system.

The Modern Era

Websites were being created at a blistering pace to help anyone with an internet connection to learn, shop and socialise online. Some of the world's biggest websites, such as Amazon, Google, eBay, IMDb and Yahoo, first went online in the '90s. Then followed the launch of Wikipedia, Facebook and Youtube. In 2008, Google launched Chrome with a focus on speed, security, and automatic updates. Today, browsers iterate rapidly and converge on standards through the W3C and related bodies, with accessibility, privacy, and performance now core priorities.

Trends & the Road Ahead

  • Deeper integration with Artificial Intelligence
  • Expansion of the Internet of Things
  • Regulations for privacy and fair competition.
